Analysis

Technical Skill Snapshot

Working as a multilingual librarian at the Los Angeles Public Library requires a mix of technical skills, language ability, and cultural awareness. Important technical skills include selecting and evaluating books for multilingual collections, working with vendors and publishers, managing databases and digital resources, and reviewing circulation statistics to understand what materials patrons are using and where the collection may need improvement. The role also involves creating outreach materials such as blog posts, social media content, and informational flyers, as well as coordinating translation so that library information is accessible in multiple languages.

Beyond technical tasks, cultural competency is a key part of the job. Language fluency alone is not enough—librarians also need to understand cultural differences, generational experiences, and trends within the communities they serve. For example, Patty keeps up with Spanish-language media, including radio and popular literature, to stay aware of what topics and titles are culturally relevant. She also makes an effort to ensure that Spanish-language collections represent a range of Spanish-speaking countries rather than focusing only on Mexican authors or perspectives. Her approach is also shaped by a strong commitment to social justice and equitable access to information, informed by her earlier work with predominantly migrant communities in Yolo County.

There is a growing need for multilingual and culturally skilled librarians, even though overall librarian employment in the U.S. is projected to grow slowly—about 2% from 2024 to 2034, with roughly 13,500 openings each year due mostly to retirements. While total job growth is modest, libraries are increasingly focused on serving diverse communities. As a result, skills in multiple languages, cultural awareness, and community engagement are in high demand.

Job postings and professional research show that language ability—especially Spanish—is frequently listed as a required or preferred skill, particularly for positions in collection development, outreach, and specialized services. Librarians with experience in translation, creating multilingual materials, and culturally responsive programming are especially valuable in large city systems like the Los Angeles Public Library, where patrons speak many languages and rely on the library for equitable access to information.

While the number of librarian positions may grow slowly, multilingual librarians with strong cultural competency, outreach experience, and skills in digital content creation or collection analysis are likely to remain highly sought after. These abilities help libraries expand access and better serve linguistically and culturally diverse communities.

Training Agenda

In order to enter this profession, an MLIS with a focus on public librarianship is required. For this role specifically, its necessary to obviously also be proficient in a second language. However, as Patty was sharp to point out to us, proficiency is a complex topic. Having a grasp on the grammar and literature is one thing; having the generational and cultural knowledge for the everyday reality of a spoken language is another. This means that not only is proficiency in a second language necessary, but there also must be a cultural element to the work. How to get this cultural understanding? Language classes and immersion help, as does working internships with other librarians doing this work, and getting a sense of what it means day-to-day. Often librarians in this particular profession are bridging the gap between generations of immigrants; this means a sharp attention to changes in the language and culture that reflect those differences in understanding, use and relevance to the library system.

Labor Trends

We are lucky to be pursuing our MLIS degrees in a city with such a large and thorough public library system! There are many opportunities for internships and jobs with the LAPL, from clerking to communications to (eventually) librarianship. The internship program is fairly robust through LAPL, especially for MLIS students, however, not many of these internships are paid. Boo! The AFCSME Local 2626, also known as the Librarians Guild LA, is the professional organization representing LAPL librarians and advocating for fair contracts.

Clerking is a good alternative while pursuing the MLIS, and it's a position which still allows for experience directly in a branch . Job opportunities are posted on the LAPL/LA City Careers Site https://www.governmentjobs.com/careers/lacity
